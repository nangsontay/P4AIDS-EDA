1.1. Problem Objective & Feature Engineering

Objective: Classify news articles into 4 main topics — World, Sports, Business, Sci/Tech — corresponding to labels 1 through 4.

Feature Creation: Since article information is split across the title and description columns, the team concatenated both into a single text field named text. This text field serves as the complete semantic input for training ML/DL models.

Initial Hypothesis: Sports and World vocabulary tend to be highly distinctive and easy to distinguish. Meanwhile, Business and Sci/Tech may exhibit vocabulary overlap — for example, articles about financial reports from major tech corporations like Apple or Microsoft.

1.2. Data Scale & Quality Assessment

Scale: Dataset initialized successfully with 120,000 training samples and 7,600 test samples.

Cleaning Results: Checked for null values and duplicates on the new text column. Result: training samples remain at exactly 120,000 rows — no data was removed.

Conclusion: The raw dataset is extremely clean and accurate. Zero duplicate records eliminates any risk of data leakage between training and validation sets.

1.3. Target Variable Distribution (Class Balance)

Analysis: All 4 labels (1, 2, 3, 4) each contain exactly 30,000 articles — a perfect 25% distribution per class.

Methodological Conclusion: Since the dataset is perfectly balanced, no oversampling/SMOTE or undersampling techniques are needed. Accuracy will be the primary and most reliable metric for model evaluation.

📏 Chapter 2 — Physical Text Structure Analysis

▼

Objective: Answer the question "How long are these texts?" to establish the Input Shape for Neural Network architectures — especially long-context models like LSTM or Transformers/BERT — optimizing memory usage and training time.

2.1. Word Count Distribution

Statistical Overview: Articles in the dataset are concise, consistent with the nature of news summaries. Average length is approximately 37.85 words, with the shortest article at 8 words and the longest reaching 177 words.

Distribution Shape Analysis (Boxplot & KDE):
• Uniformity: The Boxplot and KDE density curve show that length distribution across all 4 labels has no significant difference. All classes concentrate densely (KDE peak) in the 30–45 word range.
• Outliers: The Boxplot reveals many outlier points above the upper whisker (60–177 words) across all classes. However, this "tail" is very thin on the KDE plot, confirming that long articles are very rare.

Data Quality Finding: When inspecting the minimum outliers (articles with exactly 8 words), raw HTML tags were discovered — e.g., <A HREF=...>, <strong>. This indicates the data still contains web crawling noise. In the next preprocessing phase, Regex must be used to strip these HTML codes before passing text to the Tokenizer.

2.2. Deep Learning Input Parameters

Selection Rationale: Setting max_length too high (e.g., 177) wastes RAM on meaningless padding tokens. Setting it too low loses important semantic information.

Decision: 95% of articles have 53 words or fewer.

Conclusion: Use max_length = 53 as the standard tokenization parameter. Articles shorter than 53 words will be padded; the very few articles longer than 53 words will be truncated. This is the perfect balance — retaining 95% of dataset information while maximizing hardware efficiency.

import re, html

def clean_text(text):
    text = html.unescape(text)
    text = re.sub(r'<[^>]+>', ' ', text)
    text = re.sub(r'[^a-zA-Z\s]', ' ', text)

🔤 Chapter 3 — Lexical & Semantic Analysis

▼

Objective: This is the core of the EDA process. The team applies NLP statistical techniques to extract semantic identities, find vocabulary boundaries between the 4 topic labels, and optimize features before feeding into ML models.

3.1. Unigram Frequency Analysis & Stopword Detection

Overview: After basic preprocessing (lowercasing, removing punctuation and special characters), the Top 15 most frequent unigrams across the entire dataset are dominated by: new, said, reuters, ap, year, quot, plus days of the week (monday, wednesday...).

Cross-Label Insight: When examining Top 10 words per class, a clear pattern emerges — journalistic citation words like said, new, reuters, ap appear densely and uniformly across most labels (World, Business, Sci/Tech, and Sports).

Actionable Insight: These words carry zero topic classification value — they only reflect news agency writing style. They are classified as "Noise." Core recommendation: Build a Custom Stopwords list containing these words (reuters, ap, afp, said, quot, new, year...) and filter them out completely before model training.

3.2. N-gram (Bigram) Analysis

Contextual Analysis with Bigrams: Shifting from unigrams to bigrams, each topic's semantics immediately becomes clearer and sharper. Top 10 Bigrams reveal absolute identity-defining phrases per class:

• World: prime minister, president bush, united nations, yasser arafat
• Sports: world cup, red sox, manchester united, champions league
• Business: oil prices, wall street, chief executive, crude oil
• Sci/Tech: open source, microsoft corp, search engine, windows xp

Noise Discovery: The chart also exposes meaningless bigrams like ap ap, reuters reuters, afp afp — reinforcing the need to eliminate Custom Stopwords from Section 3.1.

Actionable Insight: Bigrams provide vastly superior contextual information. Configure ngram_range=(1, 2) when initializing the TF-IDF Vectorizer to capture these valuable keyword phrases.

3.3. TF-IDF Feature Separation

Methodology: Instead of just counting occurrences (CountVectorizer), TF-IDF measures the "importance" of a word — penalizing words that spread across all articles while rewarding words that appear exclusively in one topic.

Results (Insight): The Top TF-IDF words define the sharpest vocabulary boundaries per class:
• World: political/conflict terms — iraq, president, killed, minister, bush
• Sports: competition/awards — game, season, team, win, cup, league, victory
• Business: market/finance — oil, prices, company, percent, profit, stocks
• Sci/Tech: tech entities — microsoft, software, internet, space, music, computer, search

3.4. Lexical Diversity (Type-Token Ratio)

Methodology: Measure TTR (Type-Token Ratio) = Unique Words / Total Tokens. A higher TTR indicates more diverse vocabulary usage.

Results (Insight):
• Sci/Tech leads with the highest lexical diversity — technology constantly generates new jargon, product names, and scientific concepts.
• Sports ranks second — must mention countless player names, teams, and global tournaments.
• Business has the lowest TTR — financial reporting follows formulaic, repetitive language patterns (stock rise/fall, profits, interest rates).

Actionable Insight: High TTR in Sci/Tech and Sports requires DL models to maintain a sufficiently large vocabulary_size to avoid cutting out important domain-specific terms.

Chapter 3 Conclusion: The dataset exhibits well-differentiated vocabulary characteristics in both semantics and diversity. After removing noise (Custom Stopwords), using TF-IDF with Bigrams (1,2) and a sufficiently large vocabulary promises high-quality input, enabling classifiers (Naive Bayes, SVM, Logistic Regression) to achieve optimal accuracy.

💻 Source Code — Unigram, Bigram & TF-IDF Analysis

Uses CountVectorizer for unigram/bigram frequency analysis and TfidfVectorizer for feature importance per class.

from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import CountVectorizer, TfidfVectorizer
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns

label_mapping = {1: 'World', 2: 'Sports', 3: 'Business', 4: 'Sci/Tech'}

def get_top_n_words(corpus, n=10, ngram_range=(1,1), stop_words='english'):
    vec = CountVectorizer(ngram_range=ngram_range, stop_words=stop_words).fit(corpus)
    bag_of_words = vec.transform(corpus)
    sum_words = bag_of_words.sum(axis=0)
    words_freq = [(word, sum_words[0, idx]) for word, idx in vec.vocabulary_.items()]
    return sorted(words_freq, key=lambda x: x[1], reverse=True)[:n]

# --- UNIGRAM ANALYSIS ---
top_unigrams = get_top_n_words(df_train['clean_text'], n=15, ngram_range=(1,1))
words, freqs = zip(*top_unigrams)
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 5))
sns.barplot(x=list(freqs), y=list(words), palette='Blues_r')
plt.title('Top 15 Unigrams (Overall Dataset)')
plt.show()

# --- BIGRAM ANALYSIS by class ---
fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(16, 10))
for i, label in enumerate(range(1, 5)):
    class_text = df_train[df_train['label'] == label]['clean_text']
    top_bigrams = get_top_n_words(class_text, n=10, ngram_range=(2,2))
    b_words, b_freqs = zip(*top_bigrams)
    sns.barplot(x=list(b_freqs), y=list(b_words), ax=axes.flatten()[i], palette='magma')
    axes.flatten()[i].set_title(f'Top 10 Bigrams: {label_mapping[label]}')
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

# --- TF-IDF ANALYSIS ---
tfidf = TfidfVectorizer(stop_words='english', max_features=5000)
X_tfidf = tfidf.fit_transform(df_train['clean_text'])
feature_names = tfidf.get_feature_names_out()

fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(16, 12))
for i, label in enumerate(range(1, 5)):
    class_indices = df_train[df_train['label'] == label].index
    class_tfidf_sum = X_tfidf[class_indices].sum(axis=0).A1
    importance_df = pd.DataFrame({'word': feature_names, 'score': class_tfidf_sum})
    importance_df = importance_df.sort_values('score', ascending=False).head(15)
    sns.barplot(x='score', y='word', data=importance_df, ax=axes.flatten()[i], palette='viridis')
    axes.flatten()[i].set_title(f"Top TF-IDF Words: {label_mapping[label]}")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

# --- LEXICAL DIVERSITY (TTR) ---
def compute_ttr(texts):
    all_tokens = ' '.join(texts).split()
    return len(set(all_tokens)) / len(all_tokens)

for label in range(1, 5):
    texts = df_train[df_train['label'] == label]['clean_text'].tolist()
    ttr = compute_ttr(texts)
    print(f"{label_mapping[label]} TTR: {ttr:.4f}")

🧠 Chapter 4 — Advanced NLP Diagnostics

▼

Objective: Moving beyond basic word counting, this chapter uses advanced NLP techniques to expose the micro-structure of sentences and identify semantic "blind spots" between labels — a critical prerequisite for choosing the right model architecture.

4.1. Named Entity Recognition (NER)

Methodology: NER extraction on a representative sample (5,000 per class) using spaCy en_core_web_sm, examining entity structure differences per label. Focus on 3 entity types: GPE (Locations/Countries), ORG (Organizations/Companies), PERSON (People names).

Insight Analysis:
• World: GPE column completely dominates — reflects that world news always involves political and territorial entities (countries, capitals, regions).
• Sports: PERSON column is overwhelmingly high (3× other categories) — sports news is inherently athlete-centric.
• Business & Sci/Tech: Both dominated by ORG column — the story of economics and technology is essentially a story about corporations.

Actionable Insight: Lowercasing text eliminates the capitalization feature of named entities. Consider keeping Text Casing if using Deep Learning models (BERT/LSTM) to leverage NER features embedded in capitalized words.

4.2. Cross-Class Semantic Overlap

Insight: The Top 20 Overlapping Keywords chart shows that core keywords like company, corp, market, business have strong TF-IDF contributions from both Sci/Tech (blue) and Business (orange).

The Confusion Zone: Tech articles about Apple stock prices (Business flavor) and economic news reporting software growth (Sci/Tech flavor) create an extremely ambiguous boundary that frequency-only models struggle with. This is the primary bottleneck identified by this EDA.

4.3. t-SNE Visualization of Vector Space

Methodology: Using t-SNE to compress TF-IDF vector space (thousands of dimensions) down to 2D to observe actual cluster separation between the 4 classes.

Chart Analysis (Insight):
• Clear Separation: Red cluster (Sports) and green cluster (World) are dense and positioned on opposite sides of the 2D space — explaining why the Baseline model achieves extremely high accuracy on these two groups.
• Blurry Decision Boundary: In the center region, blue (Sci/Tech) and orange (Business) clusters are chaotically intermingled. There is no clear geometric boundary separating these two labels.

Scientific Conclusion: The t-SNE chart is "hard evidence" confirming that Business and Sci/Tech semantics in AG News are deeply overlapping. This strongly signals the need for Word Embeddings (Word2Vec, FastText) or Transformers (BERT/RoBERTa) that understand inter-word relationships rather than isolated term frequencies.

💻 Source Code — NER & Semantic Overlap Analysis

Uses spaCy for NER extraction per class and TF-IDF for cross-class overlap analysis between Business vs. Sci/Tech. Includes t-SNE projection code.

import spacy
from collections import Counter
import numpy as np

nlp = spacy.load("en_core_web_sm")
label_mapping = {1: 'World', 2: 'Sports', 3: 'Business', 4: 'Sci/Tech'}

# NER Extraction (sample 5000 per class)
sampled_df = df_train.groupby('label').sample(n=5000, random_state=42)

def extract_entities(text):
    doc = nlp(text)
    return [ent.label_ for ent in doc.ents if ent.label_ in ['PERSON', 'ORG', 'GPE']]

sampled_df['entities'] = sampled_df['text'].apply(extract_entities)

entity_counts = []
for label in range(1, 5):
    class_ents = [e for sub in sampled_df[sampled_df['label']==label]['entities'] for e in sub]
    for ent_type, count in Counter(class_ents).items():
        entity_counts.append({'Class': label_mapping[label], 'Entity_Type': ent_type, 'Count': count})

df_entities = pd.DataFrame(entity_counts)
sns.barplot(data=df_entities, x='Class', y='Count', hue='Entity_Type', palette='Set2')
plt.title('Named Entity Distribution Across Classes')
plt.show()

🏗️ Chapter 5 — Pipeline Design & Baseline Model

▼

Objective: Synthesize EDA insights into a standardized processing pipeline. Train a Baseline Model to validate the "linguistic blind spot" hypothesis from Chapter 4.

5.1. Preprocessing Pipeline Plan

Based on the data issues discovered across Chapters 1–4:

• Feature Engineering (Ch.1): Merge Title + Description into one text string to maximize context input.
• Data Cleansing (Ch.2): Use Regex to completely remove HTML tags (), keep only alphabetic characters, lowercase everything.
• Noise Reduction (Ch.3): Remove custom_stopwords (journalistic words: reuters, ap, said, quot, new, year...) to eliminate noise vectors.

5.2. Baseline Model Design

The team builds a pipeline combining TF-IDF Vectorizer (with Unigrams & Bigrams ngram_range=(1,2) as recommended in Section 3.2) and Logistic Regression.

Hypothesis: Based on the overlap analysis in Section 4.2, the Baseline will achieve high accuracy on World and Sports labels, but make the most errors in cross-classification between Business (Label 3) and Sci/Tech (Label 4).

5.3. Results & Final Conclusion

1. Overall Performance: The Baseline Model achieves 91% overall accuracy on the test set (7,600 samples). This is an impressive result for a traditional ML model, proving that the preprocessing pipeline was highly effective.

2. Class-Level Insights:
  • Sports: As predicted by NER analysis (Sports has highly distinctive PERSON entity features), the model easily identifies this class with F1-Score = 0.97 (1,863/1,900 correct).
  • World: Very good results with F1-Score = 0.91 (1,710/1,900 correct).

3. Validation of the "Linguistic Blind Spot":
  • 151 Business articles were misclassified as Sci/Tech.
  • 132 Sci/Tech articles were misclassified as Business.
  • Total 283 cross-errors — the highest error concentration in the entire confusion matrix.
  • Business F1-Score = 0.88 (lowest of all classes).

4. Future Direction: The TF-IDF + Logistic Regression model has hit the ceiling of Bag-of-Words algorithms. To break past 91% and resolve the Business–Sci/Tech blind spot, the problem must be upgraded to Deep Learning. Applying Word Embeddings (Word2Vec/GloVe) or Transformers (BERT, RoBERTa) will enable the system to truly "read" sentence context rather than just counting words.

5.4. Root Cause Error Analysis (Deep Dive)

Methodology: Extract all 283 misclassified samples between Business and Sci/Tech, compute reverse TF-IDF weights to find the "culprit" keywords confusing the Baseline model.

Culprit Analysis:
• Multi-purpose tech entities: The highest-confusion keywords are company, corp, microsoft, software, oracle, peoplesoft — tech entities that always appear alongside financial activities (mergers, acquisitions, stock market reports).
• General business vocabulary: Words like business, sales, million, quarter appear densely in error samples — explaining why a computer hardware sales article (Sci/Tech) gets labeled as Business.
• Residual structural noise: Presence of new, said, reuters, year in the error list shows these words still create "noise vectors" blurring the Logistic Regression decision boundary.

Experimental Conclusion: The Bag-of-Words model inevitably fails at the context boundary. For example: the word wireless or internet normally signals Sci/Tech, but in the sentence "Wireless company stocks fall 10% on Tuesday," the model is overwhelmed by Business features (stocks, fall, percent) and makes a wrong prediction.

⚡ Final Verdict: This confirms once more — only context-sequence-aware architectures (like Transformer/BERT) can distinguish when "Microsoft" is acting as a technology entity versus a financial stock ticker.
